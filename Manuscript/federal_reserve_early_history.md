# The Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy in the Interwar Period, 1913–1941

## Introduction

The Federal Reserve System, established by the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, was tested
almost immediately by the demands of World War I financing and then confronted with the
deepest economic crisis in American history during the 1930s. This manuscript examines the
institutional development of the Federal Reserve, the monetary policy decisions of the
1920s and 1930s, and the scholarly debates that have emerged around those decisions —
particularly the question of whether Federal Reserve policy contributed to the severity
and duration of the Great Depression.

## Chapter 1: The Origins and Design of the Federal Reserve System

The Federal Reserve Act of 1913 emerged from two decades of debate about the instability
of the American banking system following the severe banking panics of 1873, 1893, and 1907.
The Aldrich-Vreeland Act of 1908 had established a National Monetary Commission under
Senator Nelson Aldrich, whose 1911 report proposed a central banking system modeled loosely
on European central banks. [citation needed — cite Aldrich Commission report and its specific
recommendations]

The Federal Reserve Act created a decentralized system of twelve regional Federal Reserve
Banks coordinated by a Federal Reserve Board in Washington. The Act reflected deep American
suspicion of concentrated financial power — particularly fears of "Wall Street" domination —
while still providing the elastic currency and lender-of-last-resort functions that reformers
believed necessary. [TODO: Find Congressional vote tallies and key floor debate dates for the
Federal Reserve Act, December 1913]

The original design distributed monetary authority across the regional banks, with the Federal
Reserve Bank of New York — led for most of the 1920s by Benjamin Strong — emerging as the
system's practical center of gravity. Strong's relationships with the governors of the Bank of
England and other European central banks made the New York Fed the primary interlocutor for
international monetary cooperation during the 1920s. [citation needed — cite the existing
scholarship on Strong's role, possibly Chandler's biography or Meltzer's history]

## Chapter 2: Monetary Policy in the 1920s

The Federal Reserve's monetary policy during the 1920s has been a subject of sustained
scholarly debate. The period saw both the return to the gold standard in major economies
and the construction of interwar financial arrangements that proved highly fragile.

## Chapter 3: The Federal Reserve and the Great Depression

The Federal Reserve's conduct during the banking crises of 1930–1933 has become perhaps
the most consequential and contested episode in American monetary history. Between 1930
and 1933, roughly one-third of American commercial banks failed, wiping out an estimated
$2 billion in deposits. [TODO: Find precise Federal Reserve or FDIC data on bank failures
1930–1933: number of banks, deposits lost, geographic concentration]

Friedman and Schwartz's central thesis held that the Federal Reserve failed in its fundamental
lender-of-last-resort responsibility by allowing the money supply to contract by approximately
one-third between 1929 and 1933 — what they called the "Great Contraction." This contraction,
they argued, turned what might have been an ordinary recession into a catastrophic depression.

The counterfactual argument — what the Fed should have done — requires careful engagement
with the institutional and political constraints that the Federal Reserve actually faced in
this period. The death of Benjamin Strong in 1928 had removed the system's most effective
leader, leaving the Federal Reserve Board in Washington and the regional banks in a protracted
power struggle. [citation needed — analysis of the Federal Reserve's internal governance
crisis 1929–1933]

The gold standard constraints deserve particular attention. Under the gold exchange standard
that governed international monetary arrangements in this period, countries faced a fundamental
tension between defending their gold reserves (which required contractionary monetary policy
when facing gold outflows) and providing domestic liquidity (which required expansionary
policy). [INSERT: Data on U.S. gold reserves 1929–1933 and Federal Reserve balance sheet
changes during the banking panics]

Ben Bernanke's research on the credit channel of monetary transmission — developed before
he became Federal Reserve Chairman — provides an important complement to the Friedman-Schwartz
quantity theory account. Bernanke argued that bank failures destroyed information capital
(the accumulated knowledge banks held about local borrowers) in ways that independently
damaged credit allocation even apart from the monetary contraction. [citation needed — cite
Bernanke 1983 and subsequent work on the credit channel]

## Chapter 4: The Banking Acts of 1933 and 1935

The Banking Act of 1933 — commonly known as the Glass-Steagall Act — represented the most
significant restructuring of the American banking system since the Federal Reserve Act itself.
Its provisions included the separation of commercial and investment banking, the creation of
the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, and new restrictions on the payment of interest
on demand deposits (Regulation Q). [TODO: Find Congressional vote tallies for the Banking

The FDIC's establishment transformed the dynamics of banking panics by making retail bank
runs largely obsolete — depositors with federally insured accounts no longer had reason to
rush to withdraw their funds when rumors of bank trouble circulated. [FIND STAT: How did bank
failure rates change between 1933 and 1935 after FDIC establishment? The FDIC annual reports

The Banking Act of 1935 significantly restructured Federal Reserve governance, concentrating
authority over open market operations in the newly reorganized Board of Governors in
Washington and reducing the formal power of the regional banks. The Act reflected the New Deal
coalition's persistent suspicion of private financial power and the political appeal of
centralizing monetary authority under bodies subject to presidential appointment. [citation
needed — legislative history of Banking Act of 1935, particularly Eccles' role]

Marriner Eccles, the Utah banker appointed by Roosevelt to lead the reconstituted Board of
Governors, brought to monetary policy an early version of Keynesian thinking — emphasizing
aggregate demand rather than the gold standard orthodoxy that had dominated Federal Reserve
thinking. Eccles' views on the relationship between income distribution, consumer spending,
and economic stability presaged arguments that would later become mainstream macroeconomic
doctrine. [INSERT: Key passages from Eccles' 1951 memoir Beckoning Frontiers, in which he

## Chapter 5: The Return to Contraction, 1937–1938

One of the most striking — and cautionary — episodes in Federal Reserve history occurred
in 1936–1937, when the Federal Reserve doubled reserve requirements in three steps, and
the Treasury simultaneously sterilized gold inflows. The resulting tightening of monetary
and fiscal policy contributed to a severe recession in 1937–1938 — the "recession within
a depression" — that temporarily reversed much of the recovery achieved since 1933.

and the subsequent recovery has been a subject of debate in the literature on New Deal

## Conclusion

The Federal Reserve's performance during the interwar period offers lessons that remain
contested and relevant. The failures of 1930–1933 — whatever their precise causes in the
Friedman-Schwartz, Temin, Bernanke, or other frameworks — were sufficiently catastrophic
to reshape monetary policy doctrine. The Fed's responsibility to act as lender of last
resort, to prevent banking panics from cascading into system-wide collapses, and to maintain
adequate money supply growth, has been central to its operating framework since the New
Deal era. The 2008 financial crisis, and the Federal Reserve's aggressive response to it,
reflected the institutional memory of the 1930s failures.
