# The New Deal and the American Labor Movement, 1933–1941

## Chapter 1: Introduction

The New Deal transformed American labor relations more profoundly than any previous era of federal
intervention. Between 1933 and 1941, the Roosevelt administration enacted a series of labor
protections that fundamentally shifted the balance of power between capital and organized labor.
This manuscript examines the legislative, economic, and social dimensions of that transformation,
with particular attention to the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 and the National Labor Relations
Act of 1935.

## Chapter 3: Wages, Hours, and the Fair Labor Standards Act

The Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 established the first comprehensive federal minimum wage
and maximum hours standards in American history. The act set an initial minimum wage of $0.25
per hour and a maximum workweek of 44 hours (later reduced to 40), with mandatory overtime
pay for hours beyond the maximum. [TODO: Find exact number of workers initially covered — some

The political economy of the FLSA reflected deep tensions within the New Deal coalition. Southern
Democrats, dependent on low-wage agricultural and domestic labor that was excluded from the act's
coverage, demanded carve-outs that significantly limited its reach. The agricultural and domestic
service exemptions excluded a disproportionate share of Black workers from federal wage
protections — a deliberate compromise that allowed Southern Democrats to support the legislation
while preserving the racial wage hierarchy of the Jim Crow economy.

## Chapter 4: Industrial Relations in the Automobile Industry

The United Automobile Workers (UAW) sitdown strike at General Motors' Flint, Michigan
assembly plants in December 1936 and January 1937 became one of the pivotal labor
confrontations of the New Deal era. The forty-four day strike ended with General Motors
recognizing the UAW as the exclusive bargaining agent for its hourly workforce.

General Motors employed approximately 150,000 to 200,000 workers in its American operations
during this period. [citation needed — find exact GM employment figures 1936–1937 from company
records or Census of Manufactures]

The Flint strike's success owed much to the decision of Michigan Governor Frank Murphy to
refuse to order National Guard deployment to remove strikers from the plants. Murphy's
intervention represented a significant departure from the historical pattern of state governments
using police power on behalf of employers during labor disputes.

The CIO's success in automobiles triggered a rapid expansion of industrial unionism into steel,
rubber, and electrical manufacturing. By 1941, CIO membership had reached several million
workers, transforming the American labor movement from a craft-based AFL federation into a
mass industrial union movement. [FIND STAT: CIO membership figures 1937–1941 from CIO records

## Chapter 5: Race and Labor in the New Deal

The New Deal labor legislation's exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers from coverage
had particularly severe effects on African American workers in the South, where the majority
of Black workers remained concentrated in precisely these excluded occupations. Contemporary
critics, including the NAACP and the National Urban League, characterized the FLSA and the
Social Security Act's similar exclusions as racially discriminatory in effect if not in intent.

## Conclusion

The New Deal labor reforms of 1933–1941 constituted a genuine, if incomplete, transformation
of American industrial relations. The Wagner Act and the FLSA established federal floors under
which wages and working conditions could not fall, and federal protection for workers' right
to organize. These protections were, however, deeply circumscribed by the political compromises
that sustained the New Deal coalition — particularly the exclusion of agricultural and domestic
workers that preserved racial wage hierarchies in the South.

The legacy of these compromises shaped American labor law and labor markets for decades. The
exclusions built into 1930s legislation remained embedded in subsequent statutory frameworks,
with lasting consequences for economic inequality along racial lines.
